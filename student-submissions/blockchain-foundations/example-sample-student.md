## Summary

A spreadsheet, a bank ledger, and a public blockchain all store records, but they distribute trust differently.

A spreadsheet is simple and flexible, but the owner or editor can usually change the record. If many people depend on the spreadsheet, they need social trust, access controls, backups, or audit logs.

A bank ledger is managed by an institution. Users trust the bank, regulators, auditors, and legal system to keep balances and transfers correct. This can work well, but the user normally cannot independently verify every internal operation.

A public blockchain tries to make some records independently verifiable. Participants can run software, check signatures, validate blocks, and reject invalid changes. This does not mean the blockchain proves everything about the real world. It only verifies the rules and data inside the system.

The main lesson is that blockchain does not remove trust. It changes where trust sits: from one central operator toward public rules, cryptography, consensus, and network verification.

## Artifact

| System | Who writes? | Who verifies? | What can fail? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spreadsheet | Owner/editor | Owner/team | Unauthorized edits, weak audit trail |
| Bank ledger | Bank | Bank, auditors, regulators | Internal opacity, institutional failure |
| Public blockchain | Network participants | Nodes/protocol rules | Key loss, bugs, oracle/off-chain claims |
